Flexibility and self-negotiation feel identical on bad days
Healthy flexibility
Adapting to thrive
"How can I best serve my goal given what's actually happening?"
Context-driven. Preserves the spirit of the goal. The container adjusts — the commitment doesn't.
Self-negotiation
Undermining your goals
"Do I really have to do this? What's the least I can get away with?"
Comfort-driven. Dilutes the goal. Feels like relief now, dread later.
Question 1
Is this change driven by a real external circumstance — or by how I feel right now?
CircumstanceInjury, sick, genuine crisis
Internal state"I don't feel like it."
Question 2
Does this adjustment preserve the integrity of my goal — or dilute it?
PreservesWalk instead of gym. Sushi instead of planned meal.
DilutesSkipping entirely. "I'll double up tomorrow."
Question 3
Am I making this choice consciously — or impulsively?
Conscious"Given X, I choose Y because it serves Z."
ImpulsiveA mental scramble to justify avoidance.
Question 4
What story am I telling myself — adaptation or excuse?
Adaptation"Life presented a hurdle. I found a way over it."
Excuse"It's okay to break my promise because…"
Question 5
Does this flexibility have a clear boundary — or is it a slippery slope?
Bounded"One slice at my friend's birthday." Clear.
Vague"I'll start again after the weekend."
Mostly flexibility
Adjust and move. The container shifted; the commitment held.
Mostly negotiation
Name it. Feel it 20 seconds. Accept it. Ask: what's the next best decision right now? Not Monday. Now.
The reflex problem
When stopping doesn't quite make sense
The reflex always sounds rational. Protective. Mature. But the reflex is not prophecy — it's memory disguised as intuition. You don't need to eliminate the fear. You need to decide whether the reflex decides what happens next. Sometimes the sound in the bushes is just a puppy.
